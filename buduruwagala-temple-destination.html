Buduruwagala Temple

Ancient rock temple featuring Sri Lanka's tallest standing Buddha statue carved into cliff face in serene, secluded location.

Buddhist Religious Destination

Discover Hidden Buddhist Art at Buduruwagala

Uncover one of Sri Lanka's most enigmatic archaeological treasures, where seven magnificent stone statues emerge from a secluded rock face. Buduruwagala represents a unique fusion of Buddhist and Mahayana traditions in ancient Sri Lankan art.

About Buduruwagala Temple:

Buduruwagala, meaning "the rock of Buddha statues," features seven colossal stone sculptures carved directly into a natural rock face during the 10th century. Located in a secluded valley near Wellawaya, this remarkable site represents one of the finest examples of ancient Mahayana Buddhist art in Sri Lanka, showcasing influences from both Indian and local artistic traditions.

The central figure is a towering Buddha statue standing 51 feet high, making it one of the tallest ancient Buddha statues in Sri Lanka. Flanked by six other figures including Bodhisattvas and attendants, these sculptures demonstrate sophisticated carving techniques and deep spiritual symbolism. The site's remote location has helped preserve these masterpieces for over a thousand years.

What Makes Buduruwagala Special:

The sculptures at Buduruwagala are unique in Sri Lankan Buddhist art for their Mahayana influences, featuring Bodhisattva figures rarely found elsewhere on the island. The intricate details visible in the drapery, facial expressions, and symbolic hand gestures (mudras) demonstrate the high level of artistic achievement during this period. Traces of ancient paint and lotus petals carved at the base add to the site's mystique.

  • Viewing the magnificent 51-foot central Buddha statue
  • Exploring rare Mahayana Buddhist sculptures in Sri Lanka
  • Discovering intricate carving details and symbolism
  • Experiencing peaceful secluded natural setting
  • Learning about ancient Buddhist artistic traditions
Best Time to Visit

Buduruwagala can be visited year-round, but the dry season from November to April offers the most comfortable conditions for the short walk to the site. The sculptures are best viewed in the soft light of early morning (7:00-9:00 AM) or late afternoon (4:00-6:00 PM) when the natural lighting enhances the carved details and creates dramatic shadows.

The site requires a brief 10-minute walk through rural countryside from the entrance. Wear comfortable walking shoes and bring water, as facilities are limited in this remote location. The peaceful setting makes it an ideal spot for quiet contemplation and photography.

  • Rare example of Mahayana Buddhist art in Sri Lanka with 1,000-year-old sculptures
  • Features one of Sri Lanka's tallest ancient Buddha statues at 51 feet high
  • Peaceful, secluded location away from crowds in beautiful natural setting
